Introduction


Human Practices is an essential part of any iGEM project. It challenges engineers to make useful projects that contribute to society. Synthetic biology can bring innovative approaches to solve some major societal problems, however it can also raise many questions. This is where Human Practices comes in to make the teams more aware of the positive as well as the negative impact of their project. Our objective is to fully integrate all stakeholders involved into our project to end up with a therapy that will truly be a great benefit to as many people as possible.

As stated by the iGEM community: “Human Practices matters at every stage of your project”. With this thought in mind, the TU Eindhoven 2023 got inspired by the “syn-PNOIA Cycle” from the Patras Medicine 2022 team. This team from last year divided the iGEM journey into six chronological steps. A small adaptation of the “syn-PNOIA cycle” can be seen below.

The iGEM journey starts by defining and understanding the problem the team wants to tackle and ends with the proposed implementation into the real world. At every step, stakeholders are of great interest. Involvement with stakeholders helps to develop a project that is good and responsible for the world. However, not every stakeholder is equally useful at each step of the cycle. That is why the Human Practices team did a stakeholder identification to identify which stakeholders are of interest at which step. The results of the brainstorm session can be seen below.

After we discussed what stakeholders are of importance for our project at all five steps, we grouped all stakeholders into the six following categories:

  • Science and research institutes
  • Safety and ethics
  • Health care and hospitals
  • Laws and regulations
  • Business and industry
  • Public organizations and communities

We have to take into account that the stakeholders from the different categories described in the stakeholder identification section will have different interests. Furthermore, it is relevant for us to know what the focus is of the stakeholder, since in the end, they all need to be satisfied if we want to develop a successful product. To make the best design, the first step is to find out what the most important values are to our stakeholders.

We want to take a consumer-driven product design approach which has been proven to better fulfill the needs of the user than other methods[1]. This means that we will use value-sensitive design (VSD) to keep in mind and prioritize the values of our stakeholders, while also keeping track of the technological limitations of the design. This type of analysis has also been used by many other iGEM teams, such as those from TU Delft 2021 and TU Eindhoven 2022. However, we have given it our own spin.

VSD combines conceptual, empirical, and technical investigations[2]. This means that there needs to be a combination of stakeholder interviews, literature research, and a consideration of the technical limits of the product to find the best design eventually. This is not a step-by-step process but needs to be done over multiple iterative cycles to design a successful product. The process was perfectly described by Graubom et al. in 2017 and can be seen in the image below[2]:

The method helps our team to proactively consider the values of all stakeholders throughout the entire design process. For the stakeoldhers in the six stakeholder categories, we prioritized their values on a scale from 1-10, which we later on used to determine the direction that our project needed to go in. A rating of 1 means that the value is very unimportant to the stakeholder, while a 10 means that the value is extremely important to them. Moreover, it helped us to recognize conflicts between values and to prioritize which values should be integrated into our technology.

Safety and non-maleficence

The most important value that was identified is safety, which is very closely related to non-maleficence[3]. Unsafe medication could cause harm or even death. The WHO (World Health Organization) has made a framework that contains all the important stakeholders and steps for safe medication. The Strategic Framework of the Global Patient Safety Challenge depicts the four domains of the Challenge: patients and the public, health care professionals, medicines and systems and practices of medication[4]. This framework shows that every stakeholder needs the final product and the production process to be safe from the production process up and until the treatment of the patient. This value has been a central part of conversations with many stakeholders from different sectors. We learned a lot about how to safely produce drugs via the GMP process from BioConnection. Furthermore, RIVM was able to tell us a lot more about the rules and regulations when it comes to bringing a therapy to market. Lastly, the doctors and technical experts we spoke to were able to point out some points in our design where safety could still use some improvements.

Some safety issues came up in our initial design that were addressed, such as horizontal gene transfer, where a plasmid might be taken up by another bacterium which could then start producing IL-10. An improvement of our design could be to move towards yeast in the future since we could then introduce introns in the plasmid, which will make other bacteria in the intestines unable to produce the protein. More information about this can be read on our Implemenation Page.

In another conversation about safety, Zoë pointed out to us that we are designing a vessel for drug delivery, and there needs to be control over who can decide what is incorporated into that vessel to make sure no harm can be done with our technology.

Our meeting with RIVM revealed that the time the bacteria stay alive after ingestion is very important to know. The strain on the environment will be much lower if our bacteria only stay alive a couple of hours after ingestion but if they remain alive for days, they might produce IL-10 in nature which can have negative effects. Furthermore, they advised us to investigate what happens when the patient takes too much of the medication, or does not even need it at all.

Public health

Public health is another value that cannot be compromised in the design of a product. The value Public Health is defined as people with good complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity as stated by WHO[5]. Requirements for the value Public Health were explained to us by agencies such as RIVM, VIG, doctors, and patients.

To take the value public health into account, we have seen the need for our therapy to be scalable, so it will be accessible to the largest number of patients possible. Furthermore, biocontainment is an important requirement for public health as well. The living organisms that are ingested should not be able to survive outside of the body to reduce the chances of contamination. Additional research is needed to find out whether the organisms survive long enough in the intestines to come out alive. When this turns out to be the case, additional safety measures are needed. Lastly, the therapy should do no harm to the patient, which means that there should be no long-lasting side effects.

We learned from Peter from VIG that it would be very beneficial to public health if the bacteria we produce can be stored in better conditions than the current alternative biologicals, this could make therapies more accessible for people who live in places that are harder to reach.

Lastly, it is important to investigate whether the bacteria are still alive when they are passed from the intestines of the patient, because if they are alive in a sewage system, additional safety measurements might need to be taken.

Beneficence

The value beneficence requires that the procedure be provided with the intent of doing good for the patient involved[3]. Apart from that, we think that a new therapy will only be used by doctors and patients if it works better than the current accessible treatments. We analyzed together with experts and patients what points make our treatment more beneficial for patients than current treatments.

One of the greatest benefits for patients and doctors is that our treatment design does not require patients to visit the hospital often. They can collect their prescription at the pharmacy, instead of having to go to the hospital and undergo injections every 8 weeks. This will lessen the burden on hospitals and doctors and at the same time make the treatment less unpleasant for patients.

Justice

The value justice means that the burdens and benefits of new or experimental treatments must be distributed equally among all groups in society[3].

To ensure our technology can be accessed by all people who need it, we have tried to make sure our product can be produced easily on a larger scale. This is one of the reasons that we chose to work with bacteria. Furthermore, we have been in touch with a health insurance company to find out what requirements there are before treatments can become part of health insurance. Our goal for the future is to make our treatment affordable and part of health insurance for every patient who needs it.

Autonomy

Requires that the patient has autonomy of thought, intention, and action when making decisions regarding health care procedures[3]. Therefore, the decision-making process must be free of coercion or coaxing. In order for a patient to make a fully informed decision, they must understand all risks and benefits of the procedure and the likelihood of success.

To keep this value in mind, we need to make sure that we well-inform doctors who are going to administer the therapy, so that they can properly discuss the risks and benefits with their patients. Furthermore, the prescription information needs to be clearly written.

Profitability

For a product to be viable on the market, it needs to be profitable. This means that profitability can also never be overlooked when designing a therapy. For as many people as possible to have access to our product, it needs to be scalable, which will in turn also lower the production costs. We think that our solution is much more scalable compared to other alternatives such as cell therapy and that production is easier than the production of antibodies, since we do not need human cell lines.

This value is most important for the business and industry partners since if a company were arising from our project, or if another company should pick up our project, a profit needs to be made eventually. This means that the costs of production need to be significantly lower than what the customer is willing to pay for the product. To find out the costs of developing our project further, we talked to multiple experts, under which BioConnection, who do the fill and finish of drugs. They told us that the last production step of a small batch of our product will cost around 40,000 euros. Furthermore, Maikel Peppelenbosch has experience with running a clinical trial in research, he told us that a phase I clinical trial costs about 1,000,000 euros and that a phase II trial costs 10,000,000 euros. The numbers found in the literature were even higher[6].

At this stage of our project, however, profitability is not that important yet, since a good proof of concept is first needed. With that proof of concept, we want to look for intellectual property that we can use to file for a patent, which will help with profitability in the first years. However, we are aware that a patent cannot be granted anymore for anything disclosed in the iGEM competition. This means that first we need to optimize the product and find ways to patent the new parts. More about this can be read on our Implementation Page.

After this is achieved, the next step is to scale up the production process to where a high enough number of bacteria or yeast can be grown to make a product that can be distributed to many patients. The upscaling of bacterial growth has been applied before in the field of biomaterials for example and there is already literature that helps with how to scale up microbial processes[7][8]. More about upscaling and our business plan can be read in our Implementation Page.

Innovation

Innovation is a very important aspect of a project to lift off in the early phases. For stakeholders to be interested, they need to see clearly that the concept is new and promising for the future. This is mostly important for the stakeholders who play a role in the beginning, such as our university TU-Eindhoven.

Our design is innovative since there are no patents on the technology yet and the formation of an intracellular hydrogel is a very new concept, it has never been achieved before with a protein engineering approach.

Privacy

Privacy of patients should always be kept in mind when doing research on a new technology. This means that their data should be handled according to all regulations during clinical trials. This responsibility will lie mostly in the hands of the stakeholders who conduct the clinical trial, such as CHDR. We have conducted our own research amongst patients and the general public as well in the form of a survey and had to make sure to keep their privacy in mind. Before conducting the survey, we consulted the Ethical Review Committee of our own university.

Conclusion and Outlook

Stakeholders often have value interests that align with each other, however for some there might be conflicts. Safety and profitability are in conflict with each other, since making the production process safer often means that the production costs will rise as well. However, we think there cannot be a compromise on safety and that this is much more important than profitability. This is why profitability will only become important at a later stage of the development of the design.

As changes are made to the design, close contact with stakeholders will remain necessary to make sure that their most important needs are still met. And as soon as the values Safety, Beneficence, Public health, and Justice are well-incorporated in the design, it is possible to carry on with further animal and patient studies, and to perfect the production process to increase the profitability of cELPro.

Reflection

We discovered that for a project that is good and responsible for the world, you should always consider a balance between stakeholders and taking their values into account. It became apparent during the value-sensitive analysis that different stakeholders have different values leading to different design requirements. These design requirements can be in conflict and the goal is to meet the design requirements of all involved actors in such a way that the benefits for them are larger than the negative aspects. We tried to prioritize which stakeholders and values were the most important to try and keep most of them satisfied. This balance is something everybody in the iGEM community should keep in mind when they involve stakeholders in their project design. Future iGEM teams should not try to meet all design requirements, rather they should prioritize the requirements and try to satisfy the most relevant stakeholders first.
